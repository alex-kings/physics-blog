Eigenfunctions and energy levels of a quantum particle in an infinite square well

Posted on 18/07/2023

In this post, we will explore the solutions to the standard quantum mechanics problem of a particle trapped in a 1D box, or infinite square well. We'll first have a look at how to derive these solutions, and then see what the solutions look like, thanks to interactive graphs.

Derivation

We are looking for the energy eigenvalues and energy eigenfunctions of the particle. Here's the standard recipe that we will apply in order to obtain these solutions:

  1. Specify the potential energy V
  2. Apply the time-independant Schrödinger equation
  3. Use boundary conditions
  4. Normalize to find the solutions

Specifying the potential

For a particle trapped in an infinite square well, the potential energy V is:

$$V=\left\{ \begin{array}{ccc} \infty & \textup{for} & x\lt0 \\ 0 & \textup{for} & 0\le x\le L \\ \infty & \textup{for} & x>L\end{array}\right. $$

Applying the time-independant Schrödinger equation

The time-independant Schrödinger equation is the form of the Schrödinger equation that we will use here to find the energy eigenvalues En and energy eigenfunctions ψn of the trapped particle. We start with:

$$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\partial^2 \psi_n}{\partial x^2}+V\psi_n=E_n\psi_n$$

And insert the potential energy \(V\) that we previously specified. We get the following, for the region inside the well 0≤x≤L:

$$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\partial^2 \psi_n}{\partial x^2}=E_n\psi_n$$

This can be rearranged as:

$$\frac{\partial^2 \psi_n}{\partial x^2}=-\frac{2mE_n}{\hbar^2}\psi_n$$

This is a standard differential equation for which the general solutions are:

$$\psi_n=A\sin\left(k_n x\right)+B\cos\left(k_n x\right)$$
$$k_n=\frac{\sqrt{2mE_n}}{\hbar}$$

It's now time to make use of the well's boundary conditions!

Using boundary conditions

Boundary conditions are the values that we expect for wavefunctions at the edges of the well. Because the potential is infinite at x=0 and x=a, the particle can not possibly be found at the well's edges. We get:

$$\psi_n(0)=\psi_n(L)=0$$

This means that the eigenfunctions can not contain a cosine term, so \(B=0\). We also deduce that \(k_n=\frac{n\pi}{L}\) . Equating this with our previous formula for \(k_n\) gives us the energy eigenvalues.

$$\frac{\sqrt{2mE_n}}{\hbar}=\frac{n\pi}{L}$$
$$E_n=\frac{n^2\pi^2\hbar^2}{2mL^2}$$

Normalization of the eigenfunctions

Normalization is a recurring technique in quantum mechanics to find the coefficients of eigenfunctions. As we know, |ψ|2 is a probability density function. This means that integrating over all values of x must equal to 1.

$$\int_{0}^{L}|\psi_n|^2=1$$
$$A=\sqrt{\frac{2}{L}}$$

So the eigenfunctions are:

$$\psi_n=\sqrt{\frac{2}{L}}\sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)$$

Let's now have a look at the solutions we just derived!

Superposition states

Valid wavefunctions in the well can be made up of a sum of energy eigenfunctions. A state obtained in such a way is called a superposition state. Note that it is important to normalize superposition states, in the same way as we normalized base states.
