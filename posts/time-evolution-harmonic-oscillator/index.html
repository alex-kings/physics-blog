Time evolution of wavefunctions in a harmonic oscillator

Posted on 30/07/2023

We've all seen graphs of the first few eigenkets in the quantum simple harmonic oscillator, but how do pure and mixes state wavefunctions evolve with time in a SHO potential? Let's have a look!

We'll first have a look at the time evolution of eigenfunctions. Then, we'll see how superposition states change with time.

Time evolution of eigenfunctions

The classical simple harmonic oscillator is defined by a Hooke's law potential:

V=12kx2

With k the spring constant. A spring or a pendulum are examples of classical harmonic oscillators. The quantum version of such an oscillator has the same potential, usually rewritten as:

V=12mω2x2

With angular frequency ω. One can use the Schrödinger equation to solve for the energy eigenvalues En and eigenfunctions ψn of this potential:

En=ℏω(n+12)
ψn=2Lsin(nπxL)

To find how time affects eigenfunctions, we simply append the usual time-dependant coefficient e−iEntℏ :

ψn(t)=2Lsin⁡(nπxL)e−iEnxℏ

An important thing to notice is that, by appending this time-dependant coefficient, the energy eigenfunctions become complex functions.

The graph below shows how the real and imaginary parts of energy eigenfunctions of the particle in a 1D box of length L change with time.

The probability density function associated with energy eigenfunctions is given by the following:

|ψn(t)|2=ψ*ψ=2Lsin2⁡(nπxL)

Where ψ* represents the complex conjugate of ψ. As you can see, the time dependance has vanished!

The probability density functions associated with superposition states, however, does change with time! We'll have a look now.

Time evolution of superposition states
